One of the first books I had to write about before finishing it. I'm on the last 50 pages, but it's taken me almost two months to finish it. I will finish it today, but can't say I'm in a rush. I thoroughly enjoyed this book, so much to say that even the *boring* parts were great. I've never cared so much about petty melodrama than before. The way Dostoyevsky writes makes the buffoonery these characters exemplify worth the endeavor. 

To address the elephant in the room, these characters pretty much all suck. They're all rich, super entitled, look down on the lay people of the time, and are jerks to our wonderful main character. I grew to really love Myshkin, and could see why even the upper crust of Russian society saw to have him around. He's a swell guy, who cares about not just life but the meaning of life. Eloquent in speech, contemplative in thought, Myshkin is a real stand-up dude. Unfortunately, the aforementioned upper crust sees him as an Idiot due to his illness, hence the name of the novel. The characters of a story have never been so wrong as they have been about Myshkin. Shown from the very beginning to not only want to comfort the sick, but also discuss and chat with anyone around, his good-heartedness is quickly taken advantage of by the first instance of meeting the remaining cast of characters.

I wish it held up towards the end as the length really could have been shortened, but I understand to really get the whole aspect of the love triangle presented you need all the facts. Regardless, I loved the actual first day of Myshkin's arrival. Discovering who Myshkin is, along with the other cast of characters, felt natural and flowed from scene to scene without any hiccups. Meeting and learning of Nastasya Fillipnova through other characters just for her to be almost the exact opposite of everyone else's perception of her felt really smart. I think where the story got a bit lost after the first time skip. We only learn of Myshkin's true feelings of Nastasya towards the very end, so we as a reader aren't exactly clued in to how their time together was spent before she fled back to Rogozhin. It seemed to go off the rails and lacked a sense of direction, but was held together by the prose and character study of the people surrounding Myshkin, like Lebdeyev and Ippolit.  

Now that I've finished it, after reading the introduction I find it very like myself to have really ignored some of the more dreary elements of The Idiot. I found myself relating to Myshkin so much I was just as ignorant as he was to a lot of the horrors all of the side characters were experiencing. I think it might have something to do with a great quote from the intro, that the characters in Dostoyevsky's novels are mad from dealing with the "absurdity and hopelessness of everyday life." I loved using Holbein's painting of Christ after being taken down as a motif. I wasn't able to make the connection between God abandoning his son, and the way all of the father figures in the novel abandon their own children. While being hard to describe without going too deep into characters relationships with one another, the more I think about it and reflect on it the more I love and cherish my time reading it. Easy 5/5.
