Let the Right One In

It’s difficult to explain how to feel about content that disturbs you. Parts of this book where inherently disturbing and disgusting, aside from it being a book about Vampires. I even found it hard to recommend to people after admitting I was enjoying the read. After getting to certain events and characters, it became slightly embarrassing to admit I was having a good time with the story. My biggest takeaways outshine the dark, dreary, and sometimes inappropriate undertones of the entire story, especially for the Vampire genre.

Addressing the largest element in the room, the child predator character was a lot. Not only do we get graphic descriptions, but we get them from his point of view, with his internal monologue, making everything extra creepy. After working in a school for so long, anything with children starts to hit a little too close to home. It all felt a little too intense, and thematically I wasn’t able to make a connection for story relevance. Part of me likes to think Eli made him his familiar to pay for his crimes against children, but it’s never confirmed in the story and I’m unsure of what we gain as audience members hearing him describe his attraction to underage boys.

The parts I did really love about this book are the parts with deep vampire lore. I don’t follow a ton of vampire content so I don’t know if these are exclusive to this book, but the way they characterized being a vampire as a viral infection that slowly takes away the hosts personality away was a great idea. As far as I’m aware vampires are just humans who want to suck blood. Here, vampires are the byproduct of a supernatural disease that takes over the body. Pretty much any scene with Eli alone or the side character Virginia were awesome. I also thought not introducing Eli’s old master into the story was really smart. A lot of other books would relish the opportunity to throw in a major character at the end for dramatic effect, and it takes a lot of tact to not do so. Major props there.

I thought the relationship between Oskar and Eli was natural and flowed nicely through the story. Both felt totally realized and I thought giving Oskar the dark background of being a little too into murder was another good choice. Oskar himself constantly has these mental battles on if what Eli is doing is morally correct or not, without realizing he’s been fantasizing about doing the exact same thing. Again, morally challenged characters are complex and worthwhile reading. The only time it gets away from me is when that morality in question involves children- not that it can’t be done well, I just personally have never been drawn more into a book or movie by such motivations.

I know this could have been a five star rating. The writing was enticing, the scenes were well thought out and had a lot of tension. The only drawback is again, oddly placed motivations. I’ll give it a four star for now, and let it sit with me for a few more months.
